Keywords: demographic, visualisasion, student’s perception, educational games, technology education

Creating game visuals is difficult. In educational games, two concerns emerge: i) does the visual representation of the game that promotes learning and the difference in graphic style have differential influences on students' experiences, and ii) do students with different demographics (gen-der, area, and school stream) have different experiences with the game's visualisation, which affects their experience? An experiment tested EBDGS A (Abstract) and EBDGS R prototypes (Realistic). 240 Sabah secondary school students were polled. The study found that gender, area, and school stream influence students’ game artistic acceptance. “Abstract” game art appeals more to male. The study also indicated that rural students prefer “abstract” gamification than “realistic” feedback. Positive SMK students find “Abstract” educational games more effective than SMJK and EBDGS R students (Realistic). The “Abstract” style is preferable for educational games than the “Realistic” style, especially for male students, rural areas, and national schooling. These results suggest that emphasizing “Abstract” or “Realistic” styles can evoke distinct game behaviours from students and help bridge the technology education learning gap across demographics. This study can help improve learning game visuals.
