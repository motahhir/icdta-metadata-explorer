Influence of Space Limitations on Optimal Sizing of Grid-Connected Hybrid Renewable Energy Generation

Keywords: Space Limitations, Grid-Connected Renewable Energy Systems, Multi-Objective Optimization

The study explores the underexplored issue of space limitations in the optimal design of renewable energy systems. Previous literature frequently neglects these constraints due to simplifications and tool limitations. To address this gap, Pareto Front Multi-Objective Optimization in Matlab is used to examine the impact of space constraints on grid-connected hybrid renewable energy systems. The analysis focuses on power generation and key optimization metrics, including net present cost, levelized cost of energy, and greenhouse gas emissions. A mathematical model that integrates renewable energy generation, grid electricity supply, energy storage, and multi-objective optimization is developed. Simulations are conducted for both one-way (grid import) and two-way (grid import and export) scenarios, revealing distinct trends. In the one-way grid setup, power generation increases with space until it reaches a threshold, while in the two-way grid setup, it continues to grow indefinitely. Economic parameters follow unique trajectories, with net present cost stabilizing after reaching thresholds in both scenarios and levelized cost of energy decreasing within certain ranges. Greenhouse gas emissions are notably affected by space constraints, with storage costs influencing the preference for energy storage or export. Emissions decrease with increased space allocation, reaching their lowest levels in scenarios that include energy storage. The findings highlight the critical role of space allocation in the optimal design of renewable energy systems, paving the way for more efficient and effective renewable energy sizing.
